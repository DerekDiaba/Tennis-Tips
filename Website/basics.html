1. Basic Strokes

Strokes are the foundation of your tennis game. Here are the primary strokes you'll need to master:

Forehand

The forehand stroke is typically used when the ball comes to your dominant side. To execute a forehand:

  • Grip: Use the Eastern or Semi-Western grip for better control and spin.
  • Stance: Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart, with your non-dominant foot slightly ahead.
  • Swing: Rotate your hips and shoulders as you swing your racquet forward, making contact with the ball in front of your body.

Backhand

The backhand is used when the ball comes to your non-dominant side. There are two types of backhands:

  • Two-Handed Backhand: Use both hands to grip the racquet, which provides extra power and control. Swing with both arms extending towards the ball.
  • One-Handed Backhand: Grip the racquet with one hand for a more classic approach. This requires more strength and technique but offers greater reach and precision.

Serve

The serve is the starting stroke for each point and is crucial for setting up the rest of the rally:

  • Grip: Use the Continental grip for versatility in serving.
  • Stance: Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart and your weight on your back foot.
  • Swing: Toss the ball slightly in front of you and above your head. Swing your racquet up and over, aiming to strike the ball at its highest point.

    • Volley

    The volley is used when you are close to the net and need to intercept the ball before it bounces:

    • Grip: Use the Continental grip to handle both forehand and backhand volleys.
    • Stance: Keep your feet close together and be ready to move quickly.
    • Swing: Use a short, compact swing, focusing on timing and placement rather than power.

    2. Basic Grips

    The grip you use affects your control and power. Here are the main types of grips:

    Eastern Grip

    This grip is commonly used for forehand strokes:

    • Hold the racquet with the base knuckle of your index finger on the third bevel of the racquet handle.
    • This grip offers good control and is easy to learn for beginners.

    Western Grip

    The Western grip is used for generating topspin:

    • Place the base knuckle of your index finger on the fourth bevel of the handle.
    • This grip allows you to hit with a heavy topspin but may be more challenging to master.

    Continental Grip

    The Continental grip is versatile and used for serving, volleying, and some groundstrokes:

    • Hold the racquet as if you were shaking hands with it. The base knuckle of your index finger should be on the second bevel.
    • This grip is ideal for serves and volleys but less effective for topspin shots.

    Two-Handed Grip

    This grip is commonly used for the backhand stroke:

    • Place your dominant hand in the Eastern grip and your non-dominant hand in the Continental grip.
    • This grip offers more power and control for backhand strokes.

    3. Basic Footwork

    Good footwork is crucial for positioning and balance. Here are some essential footwork techniques:

    Ready Position

    Start in the ready position to be prepared for any shot:

    • Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart, knees slightly bent, and your weight on the balls of your feet.
    • Hold your racquet up and in front of you, with both hands ready to move quickly.

    Split Step

    The split step helps you transition from one side of the court to the other:

    • As your opponent hits the ball, perform a small jump, landing with your feet slightly wider than shoulder-width apart.
    • This prepares you to move in any direction quickly.

    Forehand and Backhand Side Steps

    Use side steps to position yourself for forehand and backhand shots:

    • For a forehand side step, push off your back foot and move laterally to your dominant side.
    • For a backhand side step, push off your front foot and move laterally to your non-dominant side.

    Forward and Backward Movement

    Move forward or backward to adjust your position on the court:

    • To move forward, push off your back foot and use short, quick steps.
    • To move backward, push off your front foot and use longer strides to maintain balance.
